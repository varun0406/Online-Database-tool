National Accounts Statistics of India
Description:

This module contains the following 4 sub-modules:

  1. National Accounts Statistics
  2. Factor Income
  3. Supply-Use Tables
  4. Input-Output Transaction Tables

1. National Accounts Statistics

The National Accounts Statistics (NAS) module presents comprehensive sets of data series as per the new series of NAS with 2011-12 as the base year. This section also contains the previous NAS 2004-05 base year series.

The 2011-12 base year series is updated from time to time, as and when the latest data series, both annual and quarterly, is released by the National Statistical Office (NSO), Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI), Government of India, New Delhi.

The annual series presented here covers every segment of the Indian economy as depicted in the NSO’s `National Accounts Statistics’. Thus, this module covers the following data sets, both at current and constant prices:

  1. National Income and other key related macro-economic aggregates (including Price and Quantum indices)
  2. Expenditure components of gross domestic product (GDP) at market prices
  3. Gross value added (GVA) at basic prices by industry/economic activity
  4. Output, intermediate consumption, value added and consumption of fixed capital by institutional sectors
  5. Domestic saving by institutional sectors (including household financial savings by assets)
  6. Capital formation by institutional sectors and by type of assets and by industry/ economic activity
  7. Private final consumption expenditure by item
  8. Public sector transactions
  9. External Transactions Accounts
  10. Sequence of Accounts: Total Economy and by institutional sectors
  11. Depreciation as per books of accounts at current prices
  12. Key Rates and Ratios such as growth rates of major indicators with share/ percentage/ distribution.
  13. Net Value Added at basic prices (previously net domestic product at factor cost) for Rural-Urban break up are provided for different NAS base years starting from the base year 1970-71.
  14. Gross value added (formerly GDP) at factor cost by economic activity/industry, which is derived by EPWRF.

The Advance Estimates (AE), Provisional Estimates (PE) and successive Revised Estimates (FRE) are given, as they are made available for the respective years.

Additionally, quarterly estimates of GVA at basic prices by economic activity and expenditures of GDP at market prices are presented, both at current and constant prices. For data sources and methodology followed for the new NAS 2011-12 series, see Central Statistics Office, Changes in Methodology and Data Sources in the New Series of National Accounts Base Year 2011-12,Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation, New Delhi, June 2015.

2. Factor Income and Property Income
Estimates of Factor Incomes

The value of output created out of production process is distributed to labour, capital and intermediate consumption. However, if we exclude the intermediate consumption on both sides, the value added (value of output minus intermediate consumption) is created by two factors of production, namely, labour and capital. These two factors of production have claim to the income generated out of the production process and are accordingly compensated respectively by compensation of employees (CE) and operating surplus (OS) in organized sector, and mixed income (MI) in the case of self-employed/unincorporated enterprises. The distribution of factor income is, thus, basically an allocation of net domestic product (NDP) to these factors of production. This is vital to understand the distribution of income.

The factor income series is available for different institutional sectors (broadly public sector and private sector including private corporate sector) cross classified by industry of origin (such as agriculture, mining & quarrying, manufacturing, etc).

The factor income series is available for different institutional sectors (broadly public sector and private sector including private corporate sector) cross classified by industry of origin (such as agriculture, mining & quarrying, manufacturing, etc).

Estimates of Property Incomes

In the estimates of factor incomes, the components of property incomes, namely, rent and interest, which are contained in the operating surplus, were shown separately to the extent feasible. To facilitate comparability, separate statements are included presenting the estimates of property incomes and imputed banking charges (IBC) by different institutional sectors (broadly public sector and private sector including private corporate sector) cross classified by industry of origin (such as agriculture, mining & quarrying, manufacturing, etc).

Data base for the estimation of property incomes is the same as that for factor incomes spelt out above. However, another important point in this regard is that the rent in the surveys related to rent of commercial building and equipment rented by establishment, and no imputation has been done for owned assets. Further, no separate estimates are available for building.

Data are available for 1980-81, 1993-94, 1999-00 and 2004-05 base years.

Data are sourced from Factor Income and Property Income Statements published by National Statistical Office (NSO), Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI), Government of India, New Delhi.

3. Supply-Use Tables

Supply and Use Tables (SUT) play an important role as an integration framework of the national accounts. As a key feature of national accounts, SUT provides the ideal concept for balancing supply and demand, and it is the best framework for compiling gross domestic product (GDP) at current prices. The SUT constitute a complete description of the economy, as it gives detailed information on the production processes, the interdependencies in production, the use of goods and services, and generation of income through production. After balancing, SUT provide coherent data linking output of industries as products and intermediate and final uses of these products. These tables show the structure of the costs of production and the income generated in the production process, the flow of goods and services produced within the national economy and the flows of goods and services with the rest of the world.

The SUT framework is that part of the national accounts system which focuses on the production as well as use in an economy. It reflects the production of industries in which intermediate products and primary inputs are required, showing where goods and services are produced and where they are used as intermediate consumption, final consumption, gross capital formation and exports. The most important macroeconomic aggregates such as GDP, components of value added, imports, final consumption, capital formation and exports are obtained within this framework.

Data presented in this sub-module is available from 2011-12 onwards. These data are sourced from Supply-Use Tables, published the National Statistical Office (NSO), Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI), Government of India, New Delhi.

4. Input-Output Transactions Tables

Input-Output Transactions Tables

The Central Statistical Organisation (CSO, now National Statistical Office, NSO) has brought out the IOTTs for the reference years 1968-69, 1973-74, 1978-79, 1983-84, 1989-90, 1993-94, 1998-99, 2003-04 and 2007-08, and these IOTTs are systematically arranged and presented in this sub-module. The CSO had been preparing the IOTTs for the Indian economy every five years by using the same methodology so that comparable information system may develop over the years for studying the input structures and final demand pattern.

The number of matrices and sectors covered in the IOTT had increased over the reference years so as to reflect the changing structure of the economy. For instance, the latest IOTT (2007-08) has seven matrices presented at 130 X 130 sectoral classification, as against the earlier IOTT (1993-94) with eight matrices covering 115 sectors and IOTT (1973-74) that had only one matrix in the aggregated 60 sectors form. Since the number of matrices and sectors varied from one reference year to another, these IOTTs are presented as standalone tables in MS-Excel sheet format.

Data for this sub-module were collected from the Input-Output Transactions Table (IOTT) for different reference years, published Central Statistical Organisation (CSO, now National Statistical Office, NSO), Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI), Government of India, New Delhi.
